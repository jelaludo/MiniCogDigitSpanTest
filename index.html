Two-Part Assessment

Part 1 — Word Recall (Mini-Cog)
You'll see 3 unrelated words. A short distractor task follows. Then you'll try to recall them. Designed by Borson et al. (2000) at UW, takes ~3 minutes, validated in primary care across 30+ studies.

Part 2 — Digit Span (Forward & Backward)
Sequences of digits are shown one at a time. You repeat them forward, then in reverse. Tests working memory capacity. A subcomponent of the WAIS-IV, widely used in neuropsychology.

Real clinical use: Mini-Cog score 0–2 triggers further evaluation. Digit Span forward span <5 or backward span <3 are associated with working memory deficits. Scam sites show these numbers without this context.

Why a distractor? Without it, participants can rehearse words via inner speech, inflating scores. The arithmetic task disrupts phonological loop maintenance — a key insight from Baddeley's working memory model.

Scoring: 1 point per word recalled. Score 0 = high risk flag. Score 1–2 = borderline. Score 3 = passed. In Mini-Cog, this is combined with clock-drawing (we skip clock here for digital feasibility).
Part 2 of 2 — Digit Span
Working Memory —
Digit Span
Digits will flash one at a time. After the sequence ends, type them back. Forward first, then backward.
Forward Span · Level 1

Forward Span: Tests phonological short-term memory. Average adult span: 7±2 digits. Below 5 suggests potential impairment.

Backward Span: Tests working memory manipulation. Average: 5±1. Below 3 is clinically significant.

② Withhold your score until you enter a phone number or credit card
