Semantic Fluency — Categories

Name as many items as you can from one category. This tests the integrity of your semantic network — how well you can systematically search and retrieve from organized knowledge stores.

Clinical basis: Animal naming is the most widely validated category (MoCA, CERAD). Age accounts for 23.4% of variance. Normative means: age 20–39 ~22, age 50–59 ~20, age 60–69 ~18, age 70–79 ~16. Below 15 at any age warrants further evaluation.

What counts? All members of the animal kingdom: mammals, birds, fish, reptiles, amphibians, insects, spiders, crabs, worms, jellyfish — everything. Breeds count (e.g., "poodle" and "dog" are both valid). Spelling is auto-corrected. Unrecognized words are accepted but flagged.
Phonemic Fluency — FAS

Letter Fluency

Name as many words as you can starting with a given letter. No proper nouns, no repeating the same word with different endings (e.g., "run, running, runs" = 1 word). Education accounts for 18.6% of variance in this task — it tests lexical access more than semantic organization.

FAS Test: The standard clinical protocol uses all three letters (F, A, S) sequentially. Average total across all three: 36–44 for ages 20–59, 30–38 for ages 60–79. Per letter: ~12–15 words is typical. Education is a stronger predictor than age for phonemic fluency.

Stroop Test · Inhibition + Processing Speed
Color-Word
Interference
Name the INK COLOR of each word, ignoring what the word says. One of the most replicated findings in cognitive psychology (Stroop, 1935).
Three Phases

How It Works

Phase 1 — Congruent (warm-up): Word and ink color match. "RED" in red ink. Establishes your baseline reading speed.

Phase 2 — Neutral: Non-color words ("TABLE", "HOUSE") in colored ink. Tests pure color-naming speed without interference.

Phase 3 — Incongruent (the real test): Word and ink color conflict. "RED" in blue ink. You must override automatic reading and name the ink color.

Click the button matching the INK COLOR, not the word. 20 trials per phase. Reaction time measured in milliseconds.

Clinical basis: The interference score (incongruent RT minus congruent RT) measures inhibitory control — a core executive function. Typical interference: 100–200ms in young adults, increases with age. Elevated interference (>300ms) is associated with frontal lobe dysfunction, ADHD, early dementia, and other disorders affecting executive control.

What's included

Two-Part Assessment

Part 1 — Word Recall (Mini-Cog)
You'll see 3 unrelated words. A short distractor task follows. Then you'll try to recall them. Designed by Borson et al. [1] at UW, takes ~3 minutes, validated in primary care across 30+ studies [3].

Part 2 — Digit Span (Forward & Backward)
Sequences of digits are shown one at a time. You repeat them forward, then in reverse. Tests working memory capacity [4]. A subcomponent of the WAIS-IV [2], widely used in neuropsychology.

Real clinical use: Mini-Cog score 0–2 triggers further evaluation [1]. Digit Span forward span <5 or backward span <3 are associated with working memory deficits [2]. Scam sites show these numbers without this context.

Why a distractor? Without it, participants can rehearse words via inner speech, inflating scores. The arithmetic task disrupts phonological loop maintenance — a key insight from Baddeley's working memory model [4]. Scam sites often omit this step entirely, which inflates recall scores and makes their "results" meaningless.

Scoring: 1 point per word recalled [1]. Score 0 = high risk flag. Score 1–2 = borderline (clock-drawing score determines outcome). Score 3 = passed. Full Mini-Cog combines this with clock-drawing [5] (omitted here for digital feasibility). Sensitivity 76–99%, specificity 89–96% in primary care [3].
Part 2 of 2 — Digit Span
Working Memory —
Digit Span
Digits will flash one at a time. After the sequence ends, type them back. Forward first, then backward.
Forward Span · Level 1

Forward Span: Tests phonological short-term memory [4]. Average adult span: 7±2 digits (Miller, 1956) [6]. Below 5 suggests potential impairment [2].

Backward Span: Tests working memory manipulation (central executive). Average: 5±1. Below 3 is clinically significant [2].

What scam sites do differently

The Difference

This transparent PoC shows you your score immediately, explains what it means and what it doesn't mean, and costs nothing. Scam sites:

① Show alarming animations to trigger anxiety
② Withhold your score until you enter a phone number or credit card
③ Claim to diagnose Alzheimer's without clinical validation
④ Use the same public-domain tasks without attribution or scoring context
⑤ Have no IRB approval or clinical oversight

Important: This is an educational proof-of-concept demonstrating how validated open-source assessments work. It is not a clinical diagnostic tool. No data is stored. If you have concerns about cognitive health, please consult a qualified clinician. Tests reproduced here are public-domain or freely available research instruments.
